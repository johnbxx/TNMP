A Bishop Too Many, A Pawn Too Few

How White, two minor pieces down, walked his king across the board to hold a fortress draw.

Opening
Sicilian · Paulsen
Result
½ – ½ (draw)
Length
60 moves
Character
Defensive study

There is a particular kind of chess game that rewards a careful second look — not because the tactics dazzle, but because the logic of the position bends under pressure and refuses to break. This is one of them. Black wins a clean minor piece by move fourteen, enters a materially winning endgame, and still, sixty moves in, has to shake hands on a draw. Along the way White conducts a king march that borders on the absurd, and the final position is a textbook fortress nobody saw coming.

What follows is a walk through the critical moments. We will not belabor the book moves, nor chase every sideline. The aim is to understand the decisions — where the game actually turned, and what each side was seeing.

10.The Knight Sortie

Move 10 · White to move

The opening has taken a standard Sicilian shape: Black has the classical ...Qb6/...Qc7 setup, White has developed to Bd3, Be3, and a pair of knights. Black's 9...b4 kicks the Nc3 and poses White the first real question of the game.

Position · after 9…b4 White to move

The Choice

Quiet retreats (Ne2, Nd1) are safe but passive — Black emerges with a space advantage on the queenside and no problems to solve. Instead White plays 10.Nb5!?, an attempted piece sacrifice designed to trade a knight for activity and a far-advanced b-pawn.

The forced sequence runs 10…axb5 11.axb5 Rxa1 12.Qxa1. The dust settles with White down a clean minor piece for one pawn — but with an advanced passed b-pawn, an open a-file, and an active queen eyeing a7.

Material ledger · after 12.Qxa1

White has given up Nc3; won the a6 pawn, then traded rooks. Net deficit: one minor piece for one pawn — objectively around −2. The compensation is dynamic: the b5-pawn cramps Black's queenside, and the Nc6 is now attacked by the pawn and must move.

Black's reply 12…Ne5 is sensible — the knight avoids the pawn and hits Bd3. But 13.b6! pushes the wedge deeper, dislodging Black's queen, and after 13…Nxd3+ 14.cxd3 Qb8, White has traded bishops as well. Another minor is off the board, and the position looks like it has simplified — which is exactly the impression White wanted to create.

15.The Queen at a7

Move 15 · Black to move

A Queen Behind Enemy Lines

With 15.Qa7 White parks the queen deep in Black's position — not to threaten mate, but to glue the b6-pawn in place and keep Black's development cramped. The queen and pawn together are doing the work of an extra piece.

Black is objectively winning, but tied up. The Bc8 and Ra8… wait, the rook is gone; the Ng8 hasn't moved; castling short is problematic because the f8-square needs clearing. The knight and bishops need paths, and every path runs through a square White is watching.

Black responds sensibly — 15…Bd6 16.Bc5 Ne7 17.Na5 Nc6 18.Nxc6 dxc6 — and the trades continue. White is bleeding pieces but collecting simplifications.

Position · after 15.Qa7 Black to move
White's strategy is not to equalize — it is to make equality look possible by reducing the position to something Black has to calculate, not just play.

21.The Endgame Begins

Move 21 · White to move
Position · after 21…Kxf8 White to move

Taking Stock

The queens have come off (20.Qxb8 Bxb8) and the dark-squared bishops too (21.Bxf8 Kxf8). What's left is stark:

  • White: King, Rook, 7 pawns (including a passed b6)
  • Black: King, Rook, two bishops, 6 pawns

Black is up a full minor piece — effectively a bishop pair against nothing — in a position where bishops usually flourish. But the b6-pawn is an asset that refuses to die, and White's rook has the open a-file.

Engine estimate−2.4 to Black

Plans from here are clear on paper. Black should centralize the king, activate the Bc8, and then pick an entry point — the bishops belong on long diagonals, the king belongs on d6 or c7 to neutralize the b-pawn. White must keep the rook active and the king in the center; any passivity loses because Black's two bishops will eventually coordinate.

28.Two Checks, One Mistake

Move 28 · White to move

The middlegame-to-endgame transition produced a run of logical moves: 22.Ke2 Ke7 23.Ra1 Bb7 24.e5 Kd7 25.Kd3 c5 26.dxc5 Bxe5. Black has gradually activated everything, and with 27.Kc4 Bxb2 picks off a second pawn. White's king, daringly or desperately, is on c4.

The Double Check Sequence

White plays 28.Rd1+, and Black's 28…Bd5+ looks devastating — a second check and the bishop reaches an ideal central outpost. But look at the reply.

White simply plays 29.Kxb4, winning a third pawn and leaving Black to wonder what was achieved. The checks accomplished nothing — Black's bishops are beautifully placed, yes, but White's king is now on b4, shepherding two connected passed pawns (b6, c5) with its bare hands.

The engine whispers 29.Kb5 as an even stronger alternative, but the text is already excellent. This is the moment the game's character changes: Black is still winning on material, but the position has begun to drift.

Position · after 28…Bd5+ White to move

Why this is the turning point

Black has two bishops and a pawn extra. But White's king stands on b4, defending the c5-pawn, attacking nothing, and — crucially — available. In endgames, an active king is worth a piece. Over the next fifteen moves, that king will prove the saying literally.

43.The King Walks to f6

Move 43 · Black to move
Position · after 43.Kf6 Black to move

An Absurd Journey

Trace the white king's path: c4 → b4 → a5 → b5 → c4 → d4 → e3 → d4 → e5 → f6. That is ten moves of pure king activity, through Black's position, while Black tries to push an e-pawn and coordinate bishops.

Black's plan was sound: push e5-e4-e3-e2, promote, convert. But each move is a tempo White uses to step forward. By move 43 the white king is on f6, three squares from Black's back rank, and the Black e-pawn has reached e2 — one square from promotion.

Both sides are racing. Both are about to make a queen. The question is what happens next.

The king is not a fighting piece in the middlegame. In the endgame, it is the strongest piece on the board. This game is a working demonstration.

47.Queens On, Queens Off

Move 47 · Black to move

The race resolves with clockwork: 44.c6+ Kxb6 45.c7 e1=Q 46.c8=Q Qe6+ 47.Qxe6+ fxe6. Both pawns promoted, both queens were immediately traded off. What remains?

The Deflated Endgame

After 47…fxe6, the forced liquidation leaves a surreal material picture:

  • White: King (on f6!), Rook, pawns on g3, h4
  • Black: King, two bishops, pawns on e6, h7

Black is still up a piece. But the white king is marooned in Black's kingside, blockading the e-pawn from behind while eyeing the h7-pawn. Black's bishops coordinate beautifully on paper; in practice they are pointed at nothing.

The next phase is White wringing maximum value from his king and Black attempting to trade down. Watch what Black gives up to simplify.

52.The Exchange Sacrifice That Draws

Move 52 · White to move
Position · after 52…Kxb5 White to move

Rook for Bishop — But Which One?

White plays 52.Rxb5+!, giving up the rook for Black's light-squared bishop. This is not desperation; it is precision.

The reason: with only the dark-squared bishop left, Black's winning chances collapse if the remaining pawns fall on the wrong color. And White knows exactly which pawns remain — just his g3 and h4, both of which will soon be captured or exchanged, and the h-pawn promotes on h8: a dark square, the same color as Black's remaining bishop.

After the sequence 53.Kg6 Be1 54.Kxh5 Bxg3 55.Kg4 Be5 56.h5, the game reduces to K+h-pawn versus K+B — the most famous fortress in endgame theory, when the bishop controls the promotion square.

The key geometric fact

The h-pawn promotes on h8. The square h8 is a dark square. Black's bishop travels on dark squares. Therefore Black can always either sacrifice the bishop for the pawn or, with king nearby, simply prevent promotion. White cannot win; Black cannot break through without help. Draw.

60.The Final Fortress

Final position · Black oscillates forever

Bh8 — Ba1 — Bh8

The final moves are pure geometry: 57.Kf5 Ba1 58.h6 Bh8 59.h7 Ba1 60.Kg6 Bh8. Black's bishop travels the entire a1–h8 diagonal, never leaving it.

If White ever plays h8=Q, the bishop captures. If White tries to dislodge the bishop with the king, Black's king returns to help. There is no progress for White to make.

The agreed draw is the only honest result. Black, who won a piece on move 12 and two pawns on the way, finishes a full piece up and a pawn down, unable to convert. White's king, working alone, has saved a lost game.

Final · after 60…Bh8 Draw agreed

What to Take Away

Lessons from a game that should have been won

For Black: Material is not a plan. Winning a piece in the opening is an opportunity, not a result. Around move 28, Black had every right to expect victory — but the moves that followed treated the position as already won rather than still requiring conversion. The e-pawn push felt decisive; it was actually a concession of tempi to a white king that had nothing better to do than walk across the board.

For White: When down material, the cheapest resource is usually the king. A passive defense would have been crushed by Black's two bishops in twenty moves. The king march from c4 to f6 — ten moves of naked infiltration — worked precisely because Black had no way to punish it without abandoning the only plan that might have won.

For everyone: Endgames reward knowing the names of positions. The rook-for-bishop sacrifice on move 52 only makes sense if you know that K+h-pawn vs K+B-of-the-right-color is a draw. This is not calculation; it is recognition. Study the fortress, and you play moves that look like sacrifices but are actually arithmetic.
